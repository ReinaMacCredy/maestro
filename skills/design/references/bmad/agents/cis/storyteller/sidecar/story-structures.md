# Story Structure Frameworks

## The Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell)

The archetypal 12-stage transformation narrative.

### Stages

1. **Ordinary World** - Hero's normal life before adventure
2. **Call to Adventure** - A challenge or problem appears
3. **Refusal of the Call** - Initial hesitation or doubt
4. **Meeting the Mentor** - Guidance or tools provided
5. **Crossing the Threshold** - Commitment to the journey
6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies** - Facing challenges, building team
7. **Approach to the Inmost Cave** - Preparing for major challenge
8. **The Ordeal** - Central crisis or climax
9. **Reward (Seizing the Sword)** - Achievement or insight gained
10. **The Road Back** - Returning with the prize
11. **Resurrection** - Final test, transformation complete
12. **Return with the Elixir** - Sharing gifts with the world

### Application to Product Stories
- **Ordinary World**: User's current pain/situation
- **Call to Adventure**: Discovery of your product
- **Mentor**: Your product as the guide
- **Ordeal**: Implementation challenges
- **Reward**: Success achieved
- **Elixir**: Transformation shared with others

## Three-Act Structure

The fundamental dramatic framework.

### Act I: Setup (25%)
- Introduce character and world
- Establish the status quo
- Inciting incident disrupts normalcy
- First plot point: point of no return

### Act II: Confrontation (50%)
- Rising action and complications
- Character faces obstacles
- Midpoint: major revelation or shift
- All is lost moment
- Dark night of the soul

### Act III: Resolution (25%)
- Climax: final confrontation
- Resolution of conflict
- New equilibrium established
- Denouement: wrap-up

### Application to Case Studies
- **Act I**: Client situation and challenge
- **Act II**: Solution development and obstacles
- **Act III**: Results and impact

## The Story Spine (Pixar)

Simple framework for clear narrative arc.

```
Once upon a time...
Every day...
But one day...
Because of that...
Because of that...
Because of that...
Until finally...
And ever since then...
```

### Application to User Stories
```
Once upon a time, [user persona] struggled with [problem].
Every day, they [current workaround].
But one day, they discovered [product/solution].
Because of that, they [first benefit].
Because of that, they [second benefit].
Because of that, they [third benefit].
Until finally, they [achieved goal].
And ever since then, they [new normal].
```

## Freytag's Pyramid

Classic dramatic structure for tension building.

```
        Climax
          /\
         /  \
   Rising    Falling
   Action    Action
       /      \
      /        \
Exposition   Denouement
```

### Stages
1. **Exposition**: Background and setup
2. **Rising Action**: Complication and tension building
3. **Climax**: Point of highest tension
4. **Falling Action**: Consequences unfold
5. **Denouement**: Resolution and new normal

## Dan Harmon's Story Circle

Simplified hero's journey in 8 steps.

1. **You** - Character in a zone of comfort
2. **Need** - They want something
3. **Go** - Enter an unfamiliar situation
4. **Search** - Adapt to the new situation
5. **Find** - Get what they wanted
6. **Take** - Pay a price for it
7. **Return** - Back to familiar situation
8. **Change** - Having changed

## The STAR Method

For case studies and testimonials.

- **Situation**: Context and background
- **Task**: Challenge or goal
- **Action**: What was done
- **Result**: Outcome and impact

---

## Before-After-Bridge

Quick persuasive structure.

- **Before**: Paint the painful current state
- **After**: Show the desirable future state
- **Bridge**: Your product/solution connects them

---

## Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS)

Classic copywriting structure.

- **Problem**: Name the pain clearly
- **Agitate**: Make it worse, show consequences
- **Solve**: Present your solution as the answer

---

## The Sparkline (Nancy Duarte)

For inspirational presentations.

Alternate between:
- **What Is** (current reality)
- **What Could Be** (vision/possibility)

Build tension through contrast until the final call to action bridges the gap.
